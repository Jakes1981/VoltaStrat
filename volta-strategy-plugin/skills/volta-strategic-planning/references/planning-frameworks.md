# Planning Frameworks Reference

## 1. Good Strategy / Bad Strategy (Rumelt)

The core structure for any strategic planning work at Volta.

### The Kernel
**Diagnosis:** What is the challenge? Not a goal — a frank characterization of the actual situation.
**Guiding Policy:** An approach to dealing with the challenge. What we will and won't do.
**Coherent Actions:** Coordinated steps that reinforce each other. Not a wish list — actual moves.

### Bad Strategy Signals (watch for these)
- Goals stated as strategy ("We want to reach $10M")
- Fluff that sounds strategic but says nothing
- Failure to face the actual challenge
- Lists of actions without a connecting logic

### Application at Volta
- **Diagnosis:** Ecosystem fragmentation + selection problem + distribution gap
- **Guiding Policy:** Startups first, enabled by ecosystem infrastructure; ruthless selection, relentless support for the 5%
- **Coherent Actions:** go/no-go rubric + distribution coaches + co-founder matching + Sprint + Lab vetting

## 2. Leading vs. Lagging Indicators

### Lagging (outcomes — confirm strategy worked)
- Total portfolio revenue
- Companies at revenue milestones
- Documented SME productivity gains
- Provinces adopting framework

### Leading (process — tell us if we're on track)
- Coach coverage rate (% of companies with active coach)
- Data freshness (% with company update in last 30 days)
- Builder vetting pipeline (how many in process)
- Sprint application rate
- Pulse connection scores
- Conversion rate: pre-revenue → first paying customer

### Rule
Never manage to lagging indicators alone. By the time revenue misses, it's too late to course-correct this quarter. Manage to leading indicators weekly.

## 3. Working Genius (Patrick Lencioni)

Used for team design, hiring assessment, and understanding why certain work drains vs. energizes people.

### The Six Geniuses
1. **Wonder** — Asking questions, exploring possibilities, identifying what's not right
2. **Invention** — Creating novel solutions, generating ideas from scratch
3. **Discernment** — Pattern recognition, judgment, intuitive evaluation
4. **Enablement** — Supporting others, removing obstacles, saying yes to need
5. **Tenacity** — Finishing, following through, closure-seeking
6. **Galvanizing** — Rallying people, inspiring action, moving groups toward a goal

### Each person has:
- 2 **Geniuses** (energizes, flows naturally)
- 2 **Competencies** (can do, costs some energy)
- 2 **Frustrations** (drains, creates resentment)

### Volta Team (known profiles)
- **Jay:** Galvanizing (genius) — the gap his departure creates
- **Laura (expected):** Enablement + Tenacity — "the relational finisher"
- **Matt / Yako:** Galvanizing is a competency (not genius) for both
- **Christian:** Not galvanizing

### Application
When analyzing team gaps or designing roles: identify which geniuses the role requires and ensure the hire finds those energizing, not draining.

## 4. The Go/No-Go Rubric

Volta's quarterly decision framework for startup support continuation.

### Evidence Required
- Milestone progression in Impact OS (minimum movement through market milestones)
- Execution consistency (do they do what they commit to?)
- Coach time invested relative to traction
- Quality of market feedback loops (are they actually talking to customers?)

### Thresholds (to be codified by end Q1)
- Teams that haven't progressed in 2 quarters → mandatory escalation to CINO
- Teams with <60% company update submission rate → flag for conversation
- Teams where coach time invested >> traction produced → go/no-go review

### Rule
The rubric is a guide, not a mandate. Coach judgment is preserved. But every decision must be documented with evidence, not hope.

## 5. Problem-First Methodology (SME Lab)

The framework that separates Volta's 66%+ success rate from the industry average of 5%.

### Three Phases
**Phase I — Research (4-6 weeks)**
- Define the urgent business problem (not the technology)
- Problem statement: Who has it? What's the negative impact? Root cause? Desired outcome?
- Quantify the impact in revenue/productivity terms
- Validate with 3+ stakeholders before proceeding

**Phase II — Planning**
- Set OKRs for the engagement
- Define jobs-to-be-done
- Identify delivery partners (always external — Volta doesn't build)
- Match vetted builder to SME need

**Phase III — Execution**
- Outcome-focused sprints (90-day timeline)
- Measure against baseline established in Phase I
- Iterate and document
- Transfer competency to internal champion

### Non-Negotiables
- No Phase II without validated problem statement
- No engagement without executive sponsor + internal champion
- 7.5+ on SME readiness threshold or no engagement
- No custom builds — external partnerships only

## 6. Market Milestones Framework

The progression Volta tracks for startup companies.

| Stage | Revenue | What It Signals |
|-------|---------|----------------|
| Pre-revenue | $0 | Building and validating |
| Early validation | $5K | First paying customers |
| Initial traction | $100K | Repeatable sales motion |
| Early PMF | $1M ARR | Product-market fit proven |
| Scale | $10M ARR | Ready for institutional capital |

### Key Metric
Speed to first paying customer (target: within 90 days of joining Residency). This is the single best predictor of $1M ARR trajectory.

Volta's current baseline: 7% conversion from pre-revenue → first $ within 90 days.
2026 target: 24%+

## 7. Top 5% AI Implementation Principles (MIT Research)

The framework underlying the Lab's methodology.

What separates the 5% who deliver measurable AI ROI from the 95% who don't:
1. **Problem-first** — start with the business problem, not the technology
2. **External partnerships** — buy, don't build; contractors outperform internal teams 2:1
3. **Mid-market agility** — 90-day timelines vs. enterprise's 9+ months
4. **Measurable outcomes** — ROI defined before implementation begins
5. **Deep workflow customization** — not generic tools bolted onto existing process
6. **Learning-capable systems** — adaptive AI, not static tools
7. **Executive accountability** — internal champion + executive sponsor required

## 8. Quarterly Planning Structure

### Before the Session (48-72 hours prior)
- Pull OKR progress data (agents)
- Run portfolio health snapshot (agents)
- Prepare diagnosis: what's ahead, what's behind, and why (agents → human review)
- Identify the 3-5 decisions that actually need to be made (humans)
- Prepare the discussion guide (agents provide, humans customize)

### During the Session
- Start with wins — build energy and momentum
- Address diagnosis directly — don't skip the hard stuff
- For each pillar: current state → gap → cause → options → decision
- Capture decisions and owners in real time
- Separate decisions from actions (decisions = what we're doing; actions = who does what by when)
- End with a 30-day "what must happen" list

### After the Session (within 24 hours)
- Synthesize transcript to decision log + action list (agents)
- Human reviews and approves
- Distribute to team
- Schedule check-in for 30 days out

### The Test
A good planning session ends with everyone knowing:
1. What we decided
2. What they personally need to do
3. When the next check-in is

If any of those three are unclear, the session isn't done.
