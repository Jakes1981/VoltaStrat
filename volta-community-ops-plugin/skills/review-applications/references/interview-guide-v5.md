# Volta Founder & Mindset Fit Interview Guide V5.0

## Design Spec — April 8, 2026

### Context

Jaco Burger (CINO) departs May 6, 2026. Laura Simpson and Matt Cooper will conduct founder interviews going forward. This guide replaces the V4.0 rubric with a next-generation conversational framework grounded in analysis of 18 actual interview transcripts (8 from 2026 at 70% weighting, 10 from late 2025 at 30% weighting), Volta's milestone-focused and evidence-based support model, and the current AI-first startup landscape.

### What Changed from V4.0

- 6 dimensions → 5 broader dimensions (bundling related signals)
- +1/0/-1 scoring → 1-5 scale per dimension (more nuance for edge cases)
- No commercial dimension → dedicated Commercial & Go-to-Market Readiness dimension
- No AI-native dimension → dedicated AI-Native & Technical Depth dimension
- Customer discovery buried in "Obsession with Problem" → core of Problem & Customer Evidence dimension
- Community & Pay-It-Forward (lowest-signal standalone) → folded into Alignment & Commitment
- Vision & Endgame (produced generic answers) → reframed around tradeoffs and milestone thinking within Alignment & Commitment
- Rigid question structure → conversational framework with principles and example questions
- Live coaching formalized as deliberate technique (not a section, but always intentional)

### Design Decisions

- **Conversational framework, not a script.** The interviewer explores 5 themes in whatever order the conversation naturally takes. Principles and example questions provided, not a prescribed sequence.
- **5 broad dimensions.** Each bundles related signals so the interviewer thinks in themes, not checkboxes.
- **1-5 scoring per dimension.** 25 total points. Scored immediately after the interview with 1-2 sentences of evidence per dimension.
- **Organic live coaching.** The interviewer intentionally coaches in real-time when opportunity arises — reframing pitches, challenging assumptions, introducing competitors — then observes the response. Not a scheduled section. Serves triple duty: assessment, preview of the residency experience, and mutual fit signal.
- **Flexible opening.** The interviewer develops their own authentic way of setting context and building trust. The guide explains the purpose (lower defenses, establish mutual fit, give permission to be honest) without prescribing the words.

## Interview Structure & Flow

**Format:** ~35-40 minute conversation.

```
Opening (interviewer's own style, 2-3 min)
  Purpose: lower defenses, establish mutual fit framing,
  give permission to be honest
      ↓
The Conversation (30-35 min)
  5 themes explored organically, live coaching
  woven in when opportunity arises
      ↓
Close (2-3 min)
  Next steps, timeline, questions from founder
      ↓
Scoring (5 min, immediately after)
  1-5 on each dimension while memory is fresh
  1-2 sentences of evidence per dimension
```

### The Opening

The interviewer's own authentic version of setting context. The purpose is to:
- Lower the founder's defenses so they're honest, not performing
- Reframe the interview as mutual fit assessment ("we're figuring out if Volta's model is right for where you are")
- Give the founder permission to say "I don't know" or "we haven't figured that out yet"
- Set the tone for the kind of direct, honest conversation coaching will involve

This is not a script. Jaco's version was a 3-5 minute monologue about his background, Volta's model, and the reframe. Laura's version will be different and should reflect her own style. The guide should not prescribe the words.

### The Live Coaching Moment

Deliberate but organic. When the interviewer spots an opportunity — a weak pitch framing, a too-broad ICP, a blind spot, an assumption that hasn't been tested — they step in and coach in real time.

**Examples from actual interviews:**
- Reframing Mehdi's "carbon emissions tracking" into "help companies secure loans by proving ESG compliance"
- Showing Joseph the Perplexity Comet browser and asking "how does this affect you?"
- Challenging Govinda's self-service assumption: "Will SME owners actually use a platform, or do they need consulting-led onboarding?"
- Asking Alex: "If you could start from scratch today, would you build the same thing?"

**What to observe in the response:**

| Response pattern | Signal |
|-----------------|--------|
| Immediately absorbs and builds on it | High coachability, fast learner — this is what 1:1 residency is for |
| Pauses, considers, then engages honestly | Good coachability, reflective thinker |
| Acknowledges but quickly returns to original framing | Moderate — may need repeated probing to shift |
| Politely agrees but doesn't integrate | Surface coachability — says the right things, doesn't change thinking |
| Defends, explains, redirects | Low coachability — will resist coaching |

**Critical note:** One positive response isn't sufficient if it took four probes from different angles to get there. The number of probes required is itself a data point.

**The triple purpose:** This moment simultaneously (1) assesses the founder's coachability and intellectual honesty, (2) gives them a live preview of what working with a Volta coach feels like, and (3) reveals whether the coaching model is valuable to them or whether they shy away from it.

## The Five Dimensions

### Dimension 1: Problem & Customer Evidence

**The question:** *Is this founder chasing a real problem with real evidence, or building on assumptions?*

**Bundles:** Problem clarity and articulation, customer discovery rigor, ICP specificity, early demand signals.

**Conversational starting points:**
- "Tell me about the problem you're solving — from your customer's perspective, not yours. Don't tell me about the solution yet."
- "How do you know this is real? Who have you spoken to?"
- "How many people outside your network have you talked to about this?"
- "What surprised you in those conversations?"
- "If your product vanished tomorrow, what pain would your customers still have?"

**Scoring:**

| Score | What it looks like |
|-------|-------------------|
| **5** | Crisp problem articulation from customer's perspective. 15+ unbiased discovery conversations. Specific ICP with named segments. Early revenue or strong demand signals (paying users, LOIs, committed waitlist). Surprises from discovery have shaped the approach. |
| **4** | Clear problem, validated through real conversations (8-15). ICP defined but could be sharper. Some demand signals — beta users, pilot conversations, inbound interest. |
| **3** | Problem stated but partially from founder's perspective. Some discovery (3-7 conversations) but mostly within network. ICP broad ("SMBs", "enterprises"). No revenue signals yet. |
| **2** | Problem vague or shifts when probed. Minimal discovery (1-2 conversations, or only friends/advisors). Can't clearly name the ICP. Assumptions dominate over evidence. |
| **1** | Solution-first thinking. No meaningful customer conversations. Can't articulate the problem without describing the product. "I had this idea" energy. |

---

### Dimension 2: AI-Native & Technical Depth

**The question:** *Is AI genuinely core to this product's value, and does this founder understand what that means in today's landscape?*

**Bundles:** AI centrality to the product, founder's AI landscape awareness, technical moat beyond AI, AI-native tool usage, rebuild-from-scratch test.

**Conversational starting points:**
- "Walk me through where AI sits in your product. What happens if you remove the AI layer — does the product still work?"
- "If someone with access to the same foundation models started building against you tomorrow, why do you still win?"
- "What's changed in AI in the last 6 months that affects what you're building?"
- "If you were starting from scratch today, would you build this the same way?"
- "What tools are you building with?"

**The live coaching opportunity:** Present a real competitor or recent AI development. "Did you see that [X] just released [Y]? How does that affect you?" Watch whether they've already thought about it or it catches them flat-footed.

**2026+ landscape context for the interviewer:** Build speed has collapsed — an MVP can ship in a weekend. AI capabilities are commodity — what matters is data, distribution, and domain expertise wrapped around AI. The shift from "AI as prediction/classification" to "AI as autonomous agent" means products that simply present AI outputs are already dated. Look for founders who understand this shift.

**Scoring:**

| Score | What it looks like |
|-------|-------------------|
| **5** | AI is the product's core mechanism — remove it and there's nothing. Deep understanding of AI landscape and how foundation model improvements help or threaten them. Moat beyond AI itself (proprietary data, domain workflow, distribution, regulatory positioning). Uses AI-native tools to build. Aware of the "AI wrapper" risk with a clear answer. |
| **4** | AI genuinely central to value delivery. Understands competitive landscape, can name specific threats. Defensibility thought through but not fully proven. Building with modern tools. |
| **3** | AI materially accelerates the product but isn't the sole value driver. Aware of AI trends but hasn't stress-tested commoditization impact. The "start from scratch" question reveals they'd do it differently. |
| **2** | AI is a feature, not the foundation. Could be replaced with rules-based logic for core functionality. AI understanding is surface-level. Not using AI-native development tools. |
| **1** | AI is cosmetic or aspirational. "We plan to add AI." No technical depth. The product is fundamentally non-AI with AI marketing. |

---

### Dimension 3: Commercial & Go-to-Market Readiness

**The question:** *Can this founder sell, not just build? Do they know who's paying, how much, and how to reach them?*

**Bundles:** Pricing clarity, go-to-market strategy, ICP narrowing to beachhead, sales founder presence or dedicated sales bandwidth, revenue timeline thinking, unit economics awareness.

**Conversational starting points:**
- "Walk me through how your first 10 paying customers find you."
- "What will you charge and how did you arrive at that number?"
- "Who on the team is spending the majority of their time on sales and customer conversations?"
- "You mentioned 5 verticals — if you had to pick ONE to start with, which one and why?"
- "What does the first $10K in revenue look like? Who's paying and for what?"
- "How long is the sales cycle for your buyer?"

**The live coaching opportunity:** When a founder's go-to-market is too broad or their pricing is wrong, reframe their pitch. "What if instead of selling [their framing] you sell [sharper angle]?" Watch whether they see the sharper angle immediately.

**2026+ landscape context:** AI has collapsed the build barrier. Distribution and sales are now the bottleneck, not development. Founders who reach $1M ARR treat sales as a first-class activity, not something that happens after the build.

**Scoring:**

| Score | What it looks like |
|-------|-------------------|
| **5** | Paying customers or clear path to first revenue within weeks. Pricing tested with real prospects. Specific named ICP segment. Can articulate the sales motion. Dedicated sales bandwidth on the team. Thinks in sprint-sized revenue milestones. |
| **4** | Pricing considered and benchmarked. Beachhead market chosen. Has had willingness-to-pay conversations. Go-to-market strategy is specific. Timeline to first revenue defined. |
| **3** | Has thought about pricing but hasn't tested it. ICP at category level but not specific buyer persona. Go-to-market plan exists but is vague. Revenue timeline "6-12 months" without milestones. |
| **2** | Pricing is an afterthought. Multiple verticals, no starting point. No dedicated sales effort — everyone is building. Go-to-market is "word of mouth." |
| **1** | No pricing thinking. "We need to build first, then sell." Can't articulate who pays. Engagement metrics used as proxy for commercial validation. No revenue timeline. |

### Dimension 4: Founder Quality

**The question:** *Who is this person under pressure, and will they grow through structured coaching?*

**Bundles:** Grit and resilience (specific over impressive), coachability (the live coaching moment is the primary signal), speed of learning and iteration, self-awareness (knows what they don't know), focus and discipline, team dynamics (multi-founder interviews).

**Conversational starting points:**
- "Tell me about a time this didn't go how you expected. What did you do?"
- "What's the biggest mistake you've made on this so far? What changed?"
- "What are you worst at? Where do you need the most help?"
- "You mentioned you're also working on [other thing] — if you had to choose one, which one and why?"
- For multi-founder teams: "How do you two disagree? Walk me through the last real disagreement and how you resolved it."

**The focus forcing function:** When a founder is spread across multiple ventures, use the sports analogy: "You can't be a cricket player AND a field hockey player at the same time. Which one are you?"

**Scoring:**

| Score | What it looks like |
|-------|-------------------|
| **5** | Specific adversity stories with ownership (no blame-shifting). Absorbs live coaching immediately and builds on it. High self-awareness — names their blindspots unprompted. Structured learning habits. Fully focused on one venture. Healthy team dynamic with clear roles. |
| **4** | Real resilience evidence. Responds well to coaching challenge, integrates within the conversation. Knows where they need help. Focused. Good team dynamic. |
| **3** | Some adversity stories but more polished than raw. Accepts coaching input but slowly — needs a couple of probes to shift. Partial self-awareness. Mostly focused but may have side commitments. |
| **2** | Generic resilience narratives. Requires multiple probes from different angles before engaging with coaching. Limited self-awareness. Split focus across ventures or competing priorities. |
| **1** | Polished heroics or blame-shifting. Defends against coaching. No self-awareness. Clearly not focused — side projects, competing priorities taking precedence. Unclear team roles or visible tension. |

---

### Dimension 5: Alignment & Commitment

**The question:** *Is Volta's model the right fit for where this founder is right now, and are they ready to commit?*

**Bundles:** Execution bandwidth commitment, stage fit for milestone-based coaching, program fit, vision-to-milestone connection, community orientation, mutual fit honesty.

**Commitment standard:** The founding team collectively commits at least **20+ hours/week on sales** and **20+ hours/week on product**. This is about dedicated execution bandwidth, not employment status. A founder with a day job who commits 20+20 is a better candidate than a "full-time" founder spending 60 hours building and zero selling.

**Conversational starting points:**
- "What does success look like in 12 months? What would need to happen in the next 90 days to be on track?"
- "What kind of support would be most valuable to you right now?"
- "How do you see yourself contributing to other founders in the community?"
- "What would you sacrifice to make this work?"
- "Is there anything that might pull your attention away from this in the next 6 months?"
- "Between your founding team, how are sales hours and product hours covered each week?"

**Scoring:**

| Score | What it looks like |
|-------|-------------------|
| **5** | Founding team covers 20+ hrs/week sales AND 20+ hrs/week product with clear ownership of who covers which. Evidence this is already happening. Stage is perfect for milestone-based coaching. Connects vision to concrete 90-day milestones without prompting. Understands Volta's model and specifically wants execution coaching. Already thinking about community contribution. Clear tradeoffs articulated. |
| **4** | Hours commitment is there or close. Roles defined. Good stage fit. Understands coaching model and is excited about accountability. Vision clear, milestone thinking present. Community-positive. |
| **3** | Total hours sufficient but imbalanced — heavy on product, light on sales. Stage fit is acceptable but not ideal. Understands Volta at surface level. Vision directional but thin on tradeoffs. Willing but not proactive on community. |
| **2** | Total hours marginal. Sales bandwidth is afterthought — "I'll sell once the product is ready." Stage may be wrong — too early or too late. Looking for something Volta doesn't primarily offer (funding, office space, credential). Vision generic or purely financial. Community transactional. |
| **1** | Can't commit meaningful hours. No dedicated sales effort. Clear competing priorities. Stage is wrong for the program. Looking for Volta as a badge. Extractive community posture. No answer to "what would you sacrifice?" |

## Scoring & Decision Framework

**5 dimensions × 1-5 scale = 25 total points.**

Score immediately after the interview. For each dimension, record the score AND 1-2 sentences of evidence. The evidence is what Laura and Matt reference in the decision meeting.

### Decision Thresholds

| Total Score | Outcome | Next Step |
|-------------|---------|-----------|
| **21-25** | **Strong Residency candidate** | Accept into Network → onboard at next Founder Connect Day → eligible for seat immediately (capacity permitting) |
| **15-20** | **Network — revisit in 30-60 days** | Accept into Network with specific development areas noted. Re-evaluate at next monthly cycle. |
| **10-14** | **Network — longer horizon** | Welcome to community events and programming. Not ready for coaching investment. Revisit if circumstances change materially. |
| **≤9** | **No-Go** | Personalized rejection with genuine feedback and advice. Door open to reapply. |

### Dimension Floor Rule

Any single dimension scoring **1** is a flag. Two or more dimensions at **1** = No-Go regardless of total score. A founder who scores 5/5/5/1/5 (total 21) looks strong on paper but has a critical gap that needs a conversation, not an automatic pass.

### Evidence Over Numbers

The 1-2 sentence evidence note per dimension matters more than the score itself. When Laura and Matt sit in the monthly decision meeting, the question won't be "what did they score?" — it will be "why?" Useful evidence: "Customer discovery strong — 18 unbiased interviews, 3 paying beta users, clear ICP in healthcare HR." Not useful: "Seemed good on customer discovery."

## For the Interviewer: Principles Over Rules

1. **Listen more than you talk.** The founder should be speaking 70%+ of the time. Your job is to create space for honesty, then probe.

2. **Specific over impressive.** A 16-year-old's chronic illness story with specific details reveals more than a polished narrative about "working hard." Listen for the raw, not the rehearsed.

3. **The "don't tell me the solution" constraint is your sharpest tool.** When you ask about the problem and the founder can't stop describing their product, that's a signal. Let it play out.

4. **Coach in the conversation.** When you see an opportunity to reframe, challenge, or redirect — take it. It's not a distraction from the interview, it IS the interview. The response tells you everything about whether coaching will work.

5. **One positive response doesn't mean coachable.** If you had to probe four times from different angles before the founder engaged with your challenge, that's a 2, not a 4. Count your probes.

6. **The absence of evidence is evidence.** "I haven't done customer discovery yet" is a clear signal. Don't fill the gap with benefit of the doubt.

7. **Score immediately.** Five minutes after the call, while the conversation is vivid. Write evidence, not just numbers. Tomorrow you won't remember why you scored a 3.

8. **This is a mutual fit assessment.** Some founders are excellent but wrong for Volta's model (too late stage, hardware-heavy, need funding not coaching). A No-Go isn't a judgment on the founder — it's an honest assessment of fit.
