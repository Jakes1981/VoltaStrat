Problem Hypothesis

Workshop a Problem Statement

The problem hypothesis is the foundation of everything. If the problem isn’t real, the solution doesn’t matter. This page gives you the template, the process, and a live builder to use with founders during sessions.

01

The Template

“We believe that [WHO] experiences [NEGATIVE IMPACT] when [TRIGGER] happens because of [ROOT CAUSE]. Alleviating that pain would allow them to achieve [FUTURE STATE].”

Component The Question What Good Looks Like
WHO Who has the problem you’re trying to solve? A specific person or role with named characteristics. “Gym owners with 150–300 members” not “fitness businesses.”
TRIGGER What trigger makes them aware they have this problem? A specific moment or event. “When a member cancels and they don’t know why” not “general dissatisfaction.”
PROBLEM Explain the problem from the customer’s perspective. As the customer would say it. Test: could the customer read this and say “yes, exactly”?
IMPACT What is the negative impact they experience? Quantifiable where possible. “$4,500 lost per churned member” not “members leave.”
ROOT CAUSE Why does this problem exist? Goes deeper than the symptom. “No system connects engagement data to churn prediction” not “members cancel.”
FUTURE STATE What is the desired outcome if solved? From the customer’s perspective. What their world looks like after — not what your product does.
02

The Four Risk Assumptions

These connect directly to the problem hypothesis. Every startup carries four types of risk — and each assumption maps to a different part of the foundation you’re building with the founder.

Value Assumption
“Will the target customer PAY for this product?”
Connected to: the problem hypothesis itself. If the problem is real and painful, willingness to pay follows.
Feasibility Assumption
“Can you BUILD the technology with current constraints?”
Connected to: technical capability, team composition, timeline.
Usability Assumption
“Will people CHANGE their behavior and adopt a new way of solving the problem?”
Connected to: behavior change resistance, switching costs, UX friction.
Viability Assumption
“Can the value be MONETIZED with the current runway?”
Connected to: unit economics, pricing model, market size, burn rate.
Note: These four assumptions should be tracked alongside the problem hypothesis. The riskiest assumption is where to focus coaching energy.
03

How to Run the Workshop

Step 1
Start with the problem, not the solution
Open with: “Tell me about the problem you’re solving — from your customer’s perspective. Don’t tell me about the solution yet.” If the founder can’t separate problem from product, that’s the first thing to work on.
Step 2
Work through each component
Go through the 6 components one at a time. For each: ask the question, listen, probe deeper. Don’t accept vague answers. Push for specificity.
Step 3
Test specificity
For each component, apply the test: “Could the customer read this and say ‘yes, exactly’?” If not, it’s still too abstract.
Step 4
Identify the riskiest assumption
Of the 4 risk assumptions, which is the riskiest RIGHT NOW? This is where the next experiment should focus.
Step 5
Design the first experiment
Based on the riskiest assumption, design a specific experiment. Every experiment needs: Hypothesis + Metric + Timeline + Decision Threshold.
The “don’t tell me the solution” constraint is your sharpest diagnostic tool. Founders who can’t describe the problem without describing their product haven’t done enough discovery. Let it play out — the struggle itself tells you where they need to go next.
04

Real Example: Train With Us

A case study showing how the problem statement evolved through three rounds of refinement.

Round 1
“Gym owners need retention tools”
Interview result: Only 3–6 of 24 problem signals matched per interview. Gym owners rated satisfaction with current solutions HIGH.
Verdict: Problem statement too broad. Customers don’t feel the pain.
Round 2
“Gym owners with 150–300 members facing member engagement and retention challenges”
Better signal. New questions targeting smaller gyms. Cold outreach required (anti-confirmation bias).
Verdict: Getting closer. More specific, but still needs purchase-intent validation.
Round 3
Added demo commitment question at end of interviews to test real purchase intent.
Result: 80% of MVP demo recipients showed interest.
Problem-solution fit achieved.
Key lesson: The problem statement is iterative, not one-shot. It took 3 rounds and dozens of interviews. Each round got more specific because the data demanded it.

This builder doesn’t save data. It’s a session tool — use it in real time with the founder to fill in each piece together.

The problem hypothesis is where coaching begins. Every milestone, every experiment, every risk assessment flows from this foundation. If you can help a founder articulate their problem clearly — from their customer’s perspective, with evidence — you’ve given them the most valuable thing a coach can give: clarity about what to build and why.
