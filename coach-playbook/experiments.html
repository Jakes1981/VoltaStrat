Experiments Toolkit

Every experiment needs four things: a hypothesis, a metric, a timeline, and a decision threshold.

01 The Experiment Template

We believe [ACTION] will result in [OUTCOME] for [AUDIENCE]. We will measure [METRIC]. Within [TIMELINE]. If we don't see [THRESHOLD], we pivot.

02 Experiments by Stage
Customer Discovery
Value Baseline Stage

Structured Customer Interviews
When to use — Before building anything, to validate that the problem exists and matters.
What it tests — Whether real people experience the problem with enough frequency and intensity to pay for a solution.
Example hypothesis — "We believe conducting 10-20 interviews outside our network will reveal that restaurant owners spend 5+ hours/week on scheduling."
Success metric — Problem affinity score: 7+ out of 10 from at least 70% of interviewees.
Typical timeline — 2-3 weeks.
Satisfaction vs Urgency Gap Analysis
When to use — When you need to distinguish between problems people complain about and problems they'll pay to solve.
What it tests — The gap between current satisfaction with existing solutions and urgency to switch, using numerical ratings.
Example hypothesis — "We believe current solutions score below 4/10 satisfaction AND urgency to change scores above 7/10 in our target segment."
Success metric — Satisfaction-urgency gap of 4+ points across the sample.
Typical timeline — 1-2 weeks (embedded in interview process).
Cold Outreach Outside Personal Network
When to use — When all validation so far has come from friends, family, or warm intros.
What it tests — Whether strangers — who have no social obligation — still resonate with the problem and engage.
Example hypothesis — "We believe cold LinkedIn outreach to 50 target personas will yield a 15%+ response rate when we lead with the problem, not the product."
Success metric — 15%+ response rate from cold outreach, 30%+ agreement to a follow-up call.
Typical timeline — 2 weeks.
Demo Commitment Question
When to use — At the end of every discovery interview to test willingness to act.
What it tests — Whether interest translates to commitment — will they take the next step?
Example hypothesis — "We believe 40%+ of interviewees who say they have this problem will agree to see a prototype or join a pilot."
Success metric — 40%+ conversion from interview to next-step commitment.
Typical timeline — Ongoing (measured per interview batch).
"Mom Test"-Style Unbiased Interviews
When to use — When founders are getting positive feedback but you suspect it's because they're asking leading questions.
What it tests — Whether the founder can extract real signal by asking about past behavior instead of future intentions.
Example hypothesis — "We believe switching from 'Would you use this?' to 'Tell me about the last time you had this problem' will cut positive-signal rate by 30%+ and reveal actual pain."
Success metric — Discovery of specific, unprompted examples of the problem from 60%+ of interviewees.
Typical timeline — 1-2 weeks (rework of interview script + new round).

Value-Based Pricing Analysis
When to use — When the founder has paying customers but is unsure if they're priced correctly.
What it tests — Whether pricing is aligned with customer LTV and whether churn is driven by price or value perception.
Example hypothesis — "We believe our customer LTV is 5x+ our current annual price, meaning we have room to increase price by 30% without increasing churn."
Success metric — LTV:price ratio confirmed at 5x+, or churn data shows price sensitivity.
Typical timeline — 2-3 weeks of analysis.
Three-Question Pricing Discovery
When to use — Before setting or changing pricing, with existing or prospective customers.
What it tests — The acceptable price range using: "At what price is this too cheap? A good deal? Too expensive?"
Example hypothesis — "We believe the 'good deal' price point will cluster around $X/month, giving us a 20% price increase opportunity."
Success metric — Clear price clustering with less than 2x spread in the "good deal" range.
Typical timeline — 1-2 weeks (embedded in customer calls).
Price Increase Test
When to use — When you suspect the product is underpriced but the founder is afraid to raise prices.
What it tests — Conversion rate impact of a 50% price increase on new customers only.
Example hypothesis — "We believe a 50% price increase will reduce conversion by less than 15%, resulting in higher net revenue."
Success metric — Conversion rate drops less than 15% at the higher price.
Typical timeline — 4-6 weeks (need enough new prospects for significance).
Credit-Card-Free vs Paid Trial Conversion
When to use — When trial-to-paid conversion is below target and you want to test friction vs commitment.
What it tests — Whether requiring a credit card upfront increases or decreases qualified conversions.
Example hypothesis — "We believe requiring a credit card at trial signup will reduce signups by 40% but increase trial-to-paid conversion by 3x."
Success metric — Net revenue per 100 signups increases with credit card requirement.
Typical timeline — 4-6 weeks (split test).
Freemium vs Subscription Model Test
When to use — When deciding between a free tier with upsell vs a pure subscription model.
What it tests — Whether a free tier drives enough paid conversions to justify the support cost and brand dilution.
Example hypothesis — "We believe a freemium model will produce 2x the paid users within 90 days vs subscription-only, even with a 5% conversion rate."
Success metric — Total paid revenue at 90 days, compared across models.
Typical timeline — 8-12 weeks (needs time for free users to convert).

Multi-Channel CAC Tracking
When to use — When running acquisition across multiple channels without clear per-channel economics.
What it tests — Which channel delivers the lowest CAC and highest-quality customers.
Example hypothesis — "We believe LinkedIn ads will deliver a CAC under $80 vs Google Ads at $120+, with comparable 90-day retention."
Success metric — Per-channel CAC calculated with 95% confidence across all active channels.
Typical timeline — 4-6 weeks (needs spend across channels).
A/B Landing Page Tests
When to use — When conversion rate is below target or you're uncertain about messaging.
What it tests — Which value proposition, headline, or layout converts best across 4 variations.
Example hypothesis — "We believe leading with the ROI number ('Save 12 hours/week') will outperform the problem statement ('Tired of manual scheduling?') by 25%+."
Success metric — Statistically significant conversion rate difference (p < 0.05).
Typical timeline — 2-4 weeks (depends on traffic volume).
Referral Incentive Tests
When to use — When organic referrals exist but you want to accelerate them systematically.
What it tests — Whether percentage vs dollar incentives, and monetary vs emotional rewards, drive different referral volumes.
Example hypothesis — "We believe a 'give $20, get $20' referral incentive will produce 3x more referrals than '20% off your next month'."
Success metric — Referrals per 100 active customers, per incentive type.
Typical timeline — 4-6 weeks.
Content/UGC Creator Programs
When to use — When you need to build awareness in a niche where paid ads are expensive or ineffective.
What it tests — Whether user-generated content or micro-influencer partnerships generate measurable acquisition.
Example hypothesis — "We believe partnering with 5 niche creators will generate 500+ qualified site visits and 20+ signups within 30 days."
Success metric — CAC from creator partnerships vs other channels.
Typical timeline — 4-8 weeks.
VA-Managed Outbound Sales
When to use — When the founder is the only salesperson and can't scale themselves.
What it tests — Whether a trained VA can replicate the founder's outbound process at lower cost.
Example hypothesis — "We believe a VA running our outbound playbook will book 10+ qualified demos per month at 1/3 the founder's time cost."
Success metric — Demos booked per month, cost per demo, demo-to-close rate.
Typical timeline — 6-8 weeks (includes VA onboarding).

Market Positioning Reframe Test
When to use — When growth is stalling despite a working product, often a sign that positioning has plateaued.
What it tests — Whether a new category frame or competitive positioning unlocks a different (larger) buyer set.
Example hypothesis — "We believe repositioning from 'scheduling tool' to 'workforce optimization platform' will increase enterprise demo requests by 40%."
Success metric — Demo request volume and average deal size pre/post repositioning.
Typical timeline — 6-8 weeks.
Conference-as-Validation
When to use — When you need to test positioning with a concentrated audience of target buyers.
What it tests — Whether the pitch resonates in a live setting and whether the company can generate pipeline from events.
Example hypothesis — "We believe attending [Conference] with our new positioning will generate 15+ qualified leads and 3+ pilot conversations."
Success metric — Leads generated, conversations initiated, follow-up meetings booked.
Typical timeline — 2-4 weeks (around a single event).
Multi-Market Expansion Test
When to use — When the beachhead market is saturating and you need to test adjacent segments.
What it tests — Whether the same product and messaging work in a new vertical or geography.
Example hypothesis — "We believe our solution will resonate in the hospitality vertical with minimal product changes, achieving 10+ signups from a targeted campaign."
Success metric — Signup rate and activation rate in the new market vs beachhead.
Typical timeline — 6-10 weeks.
Enterprise vs SMB Channel Experiment
When to use — When the company has traction with SMBs and is considering an enterprise motion.
What it tests — Whether enterprise sales cycles are viable given the company's resources, team size, and burn rate.
Example hypothesis — "We believe 5 enterprise outreach attempts will yield 2+ discovery calls and reveal whether our sales cycle is under 90 days."
Success metric — Sales cycle length, deal size, resource cost per enterprise pursuit.
Typical timeline — 8-12 weeks.
Pricing Tier Expansion
When to use — When a single pricing tier is limiting either conversion or expansion revenue.
What it tests — Whether adding a premium tier increases ARPU without cannibalizing the base tier.
Example hypothesis — "We believe introducing a 'Pro' tier at 2.5x our current price will capture 15-20% of new signups and increase average ARPU by 30%."
Success metric — ARPU change, tier distribution, overall revenue impact.
Typical timeline — 6-8 weeks.
