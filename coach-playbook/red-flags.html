Red Flags

Red Flags

What to watch for in coaching sessions. Organized by what you'll actually see and hear.

Flag 01
"The founder can't describe the problem without describing the product"
What it usually means
Solution-first thinking. They haven't done enough customer discovery. The product is the starting point, not the problem — and that means they may be building something nobody needs.
What to do
Redirect — "Tell me the problem, not the solution." Run the Problem Hypothesis workshop. Force them to articulate the problem without mentioning their product once.
When it becomes critical
They can't do it after 3+ sessions of trying. If the problem and the product are permanently fused in their mind, they will resist pivoting even when evidence demands it.
Flag 02
"They keep saying 'strong pipeline' but nothing closes"
What it usually means
Revenue pipeline optimism without conversion. Hope, not evidence. The founder is reporting activity (meetings, demos, interest) as progress, but none of it is converting to revenue.
What to do
Ask for exact numbers — how many prospects, how many demos, how many closed? Calculate the actual conversion rate. Make it visible. If 50 demos produced 0 closes, the pipeline isn't strong — the close mechanism is broken.
When it becomes critical
Pattern persists for 2+ months with no closed deals. At that point, the problem is either product-market fit, pricing, or the sales process itself — and the founder needs to face it.
Flag 03
"The founder is doing everything themselves"
What it usually means
Chronic multi-hatting. At $10K+ ARR this is unsustainable. The founder is CEO, salesperson, developer, support rep, and marketer simultaneously. No one role gets the depth of attention it needs.
What to do
Ask "Who on the team is spending the majority of time on sales?" If the answer is nobody — that's the problem. Someone must own revenue full-time. Recommend "Buying Back Your Time" as a framework for delegation.
When it becomes critical
Revenue is stalling because the founder is the bottleneck. Growth requires focus, and a founder who does everything does nothing well enough to scale.
Flag 04
"The pilot keeps stalling"
What it usually means
LOI/pilot friction. Procurement teams or decision-makers who won't commit. The buyer says they're interested but keeps finding reasons to delay — legal review, budget cycle, internal alignment.
What to do
Question whether the problem is urgent enough. If the buyer won't sign, the pain may not be acute. Ask: "What would have to be true for them to sign this week?" If the founder can't answer, the urgency isn't there.
When it becomes critical
3+ months of "almost there" with no signed contract. Extended pilot stalls often mean the problem isn't urgent enough for the buyer to prioritize, or the founder doesn't have access to the real decision-maker.
Flag 05
"We serve everyone"
What it usually means
Inability to narrow. Too many verticals, too broad an ICP. The founder is afraid of missing an opportunity, so they're chasing every possible customer segment simultaneously.
What to do
Force the choice — "If you could only serve ONE type of customer, who would it be?" Push for a single beachhead market. A company that serves everyone serves nobody well enough to build word-of-mouth.
When it becomes critical
They're spreading marketing and sales across 3+ segments with real traction in none. Resources are diluted, messaging is generic, and the company looks different to every prospect.
Flag 06
"Team issues keep coming up"
What it usually means
Team fragility. Unpaid members, absent co-founders, key person dependency. When team problems recur session after session, the company's foundation is unstable — not the market, not the product, the team.
What to do
Probe team structure — who's full-time? Who's paid? What happens if [key person] leaves? Map the single points of failure. If the company depends on one person who isn't fully committed, that's an existential risk.
When it becomes critical
The company depends on a single person who isn't fully committed. When that person leaves — and they will — the company stalls or dies. This is especially dangerous with unpaid co-founders or part-time technical leads.
Flag 07
"The founder seems off today"
What it usually means
Emotional state as a leading indicator. Founder wellness IS a business variable. A founder who is burned out, distracted, or losing conviction will make worse decisions — and it shows up in sessions before it shows up in metrics.
What to do
Ask directly — "How are you doing? Not the company — you." Share your own experiences if appropriate. Sometimes the most valuable coaching session is a human conversation, not a strategy session. Recommend "Buying Back Your Time" if they're drowning in operational tasks.
When it becomes critical
Pattern across 2+ sessions. May signal burnout, personal issues, or loss of conviction. A founder who has quietly stopped believing in the company will coast rather than pivot, and the company will bleed out slowly.
Flag 08
"They're targeting governments or enterprises with 2 people"
What it usually means
Enterprise dependency without enterprise resources. 6+ month sales cycles with a team that can't survive the wait. The company is chasing large, slow-moving customers that require security reviews, procurement processes, and relationship management that a 2-person team cannot deliver.
What to do
Challenge the go-to-market — can they start with a smaller, faster-moving customer segment? Test with SMBs first. Use early SMB revenue to fund the longer enterprise play later. The question isn't "Can you sell to enterprises?" — it's "Can you survive the sales cycle?"
When it becomes critical
Burn rate vs timeline mismatch — they'll run out of money before the enterprise deal closes. If the runway is 8 months and the sales cycle is 9, the math doesn't work. No amount of optimism changes arithmetic.
Remember

Red flags are gifts. They tell you where to focus before the problem becomes a crisis. Name them early, probe them directly, and don't wait for the founder to bring them up.
