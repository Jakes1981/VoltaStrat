Stage Guide

Where is your founder?

Select the milestone stage. See the coaching focus, key questions, experiments, traps, and red flags for that exact point in the journey.

Stage 1 · Pre-revenue
Value Baseline
Validate that the problem exists and someone will pay to solve it. Everything at this stage is about evidence — does the problem statement hold up when tested against real customers?
◆ Coaching Objective

Help the founder move from assumption to evidence. Your job is to prevent premature building by insisting on customer-validated proof that the problem is real, urgent, and worth solving. Push relentlessly on "show me the evidence" before any solution work begins.

⚠ Primary Risk Focus

90% Value Risk — "Does anyone actually have this problem?"

At this stage, feasibility (can you build it?) and viability (can you monetize it?) are secondary. The existential question is whether the problem is real and urgent enough that someone will pay.

? Key Questions
  • Tell me about the problem from your customer's perspective — not yours. Don't tell me about the solution yet.
  • How many people outside your network have you spoken to about this?
  • What surprised you in those conversations?
  • If your product vanished tomorrow, what pain would your customers still have?
  • Would they commit to seeing a demo? (intent test)
⚙ Experiments to Prescribe
  • Structured customer interviews (10-20) — Outside the founder's network, with problem affinity scoring to quantify signal
  • Satisfaction vs urgency gap analysis — Numerical ratings reveal whether customers are dissatisfied enough to change behavior
  • Cold outreach outside personal network — Anti-confirmation-bias test; if only warm contacts validate, the signal is weak
  • Demo commitment question — At the end of each interview, ask: "Would you commit to seeing a demo?" Real intent separates politeness from demand
  • "Mom Test"-style unbiased interviews — Questions structured to prevent the founder from pitching; focus on the customer's world, not the product
⚠ Common Traps
Solution-first thinking
What it looks like: Founder describes the product before the problem. Every conversation starts with features.
Redirect: "Tell me the problem, not the solution."
Network-only validation
What it looks like: All interviews are friends, family, warm contacts. Everyone is "excited."
Push for cold outreach. Warm validation is unreliable.
Building before validating
What it looks like: "Let me just build the MVP first" — code replaces conversation.
Block this. Validate the problem first. Building without evidence wastes months.
Scope creep
What it looks like: Targeting too many segments. "It's for everyone."
Force narrowing: "If you could only serve ONE type of customer, who?"
Confusing interest with willingness to pay
What it looks like: "People said it was interesting" presented as validation.
Interest is not demand. Push for commitment signals: would they pay? Would they demo?
⚑ Red Flags
  • Can't describe the problem without describing the product
  • Fewer than 5 customer conversations
  • All conversations within personal network
  • High customer satisfaction with existing solutions (gap analysis shows no pain)
  • Problem statement changes every session without converging
✓ Ready to Advance When...
  • 80%+ of discovery conversations show genuine interest and demo commitment
  • Specific ICP identified (not a category — a named segment)
  • Problem articulated from the customer's perspective (they'd say "yes, exactly")
  • At least some conversations outside personal network

Stage 2 · First $
First Paying Customer
Prove that someone will exchange money for this solution. Not a favor, not a discount, not a pilot — real revenue from a customer who chose to pay because the value was clear.
◆ Coaching Objective

Guide the founder from "people want this" to "someone paid for this." Your job is to push through the emotional barrier of asking for money, ensure pricing reflects real value, and validate that the first sale is repeatable — not a one-off favor.

⚠ Primary Risk Focus

Value → Viability shift

Value risk is partially resolved (someone cares), but now viability risk rises: "Can the economics work? Is the pricing right? Is this repeatable or a one-off?"

? Key Questions
  • What did the customer actually pay for? What exact value did they receive?
  • How did they find you? Could that channel work again?
  • What will you charge and how did you arrive at that number?
  • What's the lifetime value of a customer vs the cost if they churn?
  • Walk me through your first 10 sales — how do they happen?
⚙ Experiments to Prescribe
  • Value-based pricing analysis — Customer lifetime value vs churn cost (TWU used $4,500 LTV vs $600 churn value as a benchmark)
  • Beta program with 3-5 named accounts — Not "open beta" — specific, named customers who commit to provide feedback
  • Channel experiments — Which distribution method works? Direct sales, content marketing, referrals? Test 2-3 and measure conversion
  • Sales process documentation — Can the founder describe the steps from "interested" to "paying"? If not, the process isn't repeatable
⚠ Common Traps
Underpricing to land the first customer
What it looks like: Giving away value to say "we have a customer." Discounts so steep they're effectively free.
Price reflects value. If the product solves a real problem, charge for it.
Building features instead of selling
What it looks like: "Just one more feature and then we'll sell." Endless product work, zero revenue.
Sell what you have now. Ship, learn, iterate.
One-off revenue disguised as recurring
What it looks like: A single project payment counted as "revenue." No subscription or repeat model.
Push for subscription/recurring model. One payment is a project, not a business.
Manual fulfillment at unsustainable rates
What it looks like: Working 80 hours to serve 3 customers. Founder IS the product.
Flag this early. Technology leverage must enter the picture before scaling.
⚑ Red Flags
  • The only paying customer is from the founder's personal network
  • Revenue is one-time, not recurring
  • Pricing has never been tested — founder guessed a number
  • Founder is manually delivering the entire product (no technology leverage)
  • Customer was acquired through a favor or discount, not product value
✓ Ready to Advance When...
  • Multiple paying customers through a repeatable process
  • Revenue is recurring (not one-off)
  • Unit economics showing a positive trajectory (even if not yet profitable)
  • Sales process can be described step-by-step

Stage 3 · $10K ARR
X Stage
Build a repeatable growth engine. The product works. People pay. Now: can you grow consistently at 20%+ month-over-month?
◆ Coaching Objective

Shift from "can we sell?" to "can we grow?" Your job is to help the founder identify and double down on the primary acquisition channel, track per-channel CAC, and build the growth engine that will carry them to $100K ARR. Challenge any channel strategy that doesn't include measurement.

⚠ Primary Risk Focus

Feasibility + Viability

Feasibility: "Can the team scale operations to support growth?" Viability: "Do the unit economics work at scale?" Both risks rise simultaneously as the company tries to grow beyond founder-led sales.

? Key Questions
  • What's your growth rate? Is it 20%+ month-over-month?
  • What's your primary acquisition channel? What's the CAC per channel?
  • Who on the team spends the majority of their time on sales?
  • What's your CAC:LTV ratio?
  • If you had to pick ONE channel to double down on, which and why?
⚙ Experiments to Prescribe
  • Multi-channel growth experiments — Per-channel CAC tracking (Optiml tracked Reddit $426, Google $390 to compare channel efficiency)
  • A/B landing page tests — 4 variations measuring conversion rate changes; statistical significance matters
  • Referral program tests — Percentage vs dollar incentives; monetary vs emotional rewards; measure which drives more referrals
  • Content/UGC experiments — Creator programs (Studley AI's $20/video + $1 CPM model as a benchmark)
  • Sales outreach experiments — VA-managed outbound campaigns to test scalability beyond founder-led sales
⚠ Common Traps
Spreading across too many channels
What it looks like: Running ads on 5 platforms, posting on 3 social networks, doing events — no per-channel CAC tracking.
Pick 2 channels max. Measure CAC per channel. Kill what doesn't work.
Hiring too early
What it looks like: Burning cash on roles before the growth engine is proven. "We need a marketing person."
Prove the channel works first, then hire to scale it.
Ignoring churn while chasing acquisition
What it looks like: New customers masking attrition. Net revenue looks flat or slightly up.
Measure churn separately. Acquisition without retention is a leaky bucket.
No dedicated sales bandwidth
What it looks like: Founder still building features instead of selling. "I'll sell next month."
Someone must spend the majority of their time on sales. Now.
Revenue concentrated in 1-2 large customers
What it looks like: $8K of $10K ARR comes from one client. That's fragile, not a growth engine.
Diversify the customer base before scaling. Concentration risk is existential.
⚑ Red Flags
  • Growth rate consistently below 20% MoM
  • High churn offsetting new customer acquisition
  • Founder still doing everything themselves (no delegation or team structure)
  • Revenue concentrated in a few customers
  • Can't articulate the primary growth channel
✓ Ready to Advance When...
  • 20%+ MoM sustained growth over 3+ months
  • Primary acquisition channel clearly identified with measurable CAC
  • CAC:LTV ratio is healthy (at minimum 3:1)
  • Team roles beginning to define (someone on sales, someone on product)
  • Churn is measured and manageable

Stage 4 · $100K ARR
10X Stage
Prove this is a scalable venture, not a lifestyle business. At $100K ARR, the founder faces an identity question: "Am I building something that can grow beyond me?"
◆ Coaching Objective

Help the founder transition from operator to leader. Your job shifts from refining tactics to challenging strategic thinking: market positioning, team building, fundraising readiness, and the crucial lifestyle-vs-venture decision. Consider a Foundation Sprint if the company needs a strategic reset.

⚠ Primary Risk Focus

Viability + Usability

Viability: "Is this a venture-scale market?" Usability: "Can the product serve a larger user base without breaking?" Both matter because growth at this level exposes every weakness in the business model and product architecture.

? Key Questions
  • Are you building a lifestyle business or a venture? Which do you actually want?
  • How would you describe your market position in one sentence?
  • What's your hiring plan? Who's your first non-founder hire?
  • Are you investment-ready? Should you be?
  • What would break if you 10x'd your users tomorrow?
⚙ Experiments to Prescribe
  • Market positioning reframe tests — Writing Battle reframed from "EdTech" to "gamification/esports for creatives" and unlocked a new investor narrative
  • First non-founder hire experiments — What role has the highest leverage? Test before committing to full-time headcount
  • Conference/pitch experiments — Investor outreach and market validation at scale; measure response rates and follow-up conversion
  • Pricing tier expansion — Can you serve different segments at different price points? Test willingness to pay across segments
⚠ Common Traps
Founder burnout
What it looks like: They've been sprinting for months. Sessions become emotional. Energy is low.
"Buying Back Your Time" (Dan Martell) framework. Delegate, automate, eliminate.
Not delegating
What it looks like: Trying to scale by working harder instead of building a team. 80-hour weeks.
Identify the highest-leverage hire. The founder must stop being the bottleneck.
Identity crisis
What it looks like: "Am I a tech company or a service company?" Positioning confusion hurts fundraising and hiring.
Consider a Foundation Sprint or structured positioning exercise. Clarity unlocks everything downstream.
Ignoring unit economics
What it looks like: Top-line revenue looks good but margins are thin or negative. "We'll figure out profitability later."
Unit economics must work before scaling. Growth on negative margins accelerates failure.
Resistance to strategic planning
What it looks like: "I'm too busy executing to plan." Reactive mode. No roadmap beyond this quarter.
Schedule the strategic session. Execution without direction compounds wasted effort.
⚑ Red Flags
  • Founder showing burnout symptoms (emotional conversations, missed sessions)
  • Revenue growth coming from non-scalable activities (manual fulfillment, one-off deals)
  • No team beyond founders
  • Resistance to strategic reflection or structured planning exercises
✓ Ready to Advance When...
  • Sustainable growth engine running without founder as sole executor
  • Clear market position that can be communicated in one sentence
  • Team beyond founders (at least 1 non-founder hire)
  • Investment readiness OR a clear path to profitability without investment

Stage 5 · Graduation
$1M ARR
Graduation preparation. The founder has built a real company. Coaching shifts to institutional readiness — board composition, organizational design, fundraising strategy, or sustained profitability path.
◆ Coaching Objective

Prepare the founder for life beyond Volta coaching. Your job is to ensure the company has the institutional infrastructure to thrive independently — governance, team, advisors, and a clear strategic direction. The goal is a clean handoff, not a dependency.

⚠ Primary Risk Focus

Institutional readiness

New risks emerge — team scaling, governance, board composition. The original 4 risks (value, feasibility, viability, usability) should all be low. If they're not, the company may not actually be at $1M ARR sustainably.

? Key Questions
  • What's your board composition? Who advises you?
  • What does the next 12 months look like? Fundraise or bootstrap?
  • What organizational gaps do you need to fill?
  • What would happen if you stepped away for 2 weeks?
⚙ Experiments to Prescribe
  • Investor outreach experiments — Test investor thesis fit, measure response rates, refine the pitch based on feedback patterns
  • Organizational structure design — Roles, reporting lines, decision rights. Document who owns what before the team doubles
  • Advisory board formation — Identify 3-5 advisors who fill specific gaps (industry, technical, fundraising, governance)
⚠ Common Traps
Coaching dependency
What it looks like: Still needs weekly sessions to make decisions. Can't operate without the coach's input.
Reduce cadence deliberately. The company must be self-sustaining.
Governance gap
What it looks like: No board, no advisors, founder making all decisions alone at $1M scale.
Form an advisory board. Introduce governance structures before they're needed, not after.
Founder as single point of failure
What it looks like: If the founder takes a vacation, the company stalls. No delegation, no second-in-command.
The "2-week test" — if the company can't survive 2 weeks without the founder, the org structure has a critical flaw.
⚑ Red Flags
  • Still needs weekly coaching to function — something deeper is wrong
  • Original 4 risks (value, feasibility, viability, usability) are not resolved
  • No team beyond the founder at $1M ARR
  • No advisory or governance structure in place
  • Founder cannot step away for even a week
✓ Graduation Criteria
  • Company operates independently of Volta coaching
  • Board or advisory structure in place
  • Clear 12-month strategic plan (fundraise or bootstrap path)
  • Organizational design supports continued growth
